Why computing has an environmental problem

We tend to think of software as weightless — a click here, a file there. But every digital action demands physical infrastructure: servers humming in giant warehouses, cables crossing ocean floors, and devices manufactured from rare metals mined at great cost to ecosystems.

The ICT sector now produces roughly as much carbon as the global aviation industry. As AI, streaming, cloud computing and the Internet of Things all scale exponentially, these numbers are set to climb sharply through the 2030s.

The key issues

ENERGY

Data Centre Power Consumption

Data centres host the servers that run our websites, apps, streaming services and AI models. They require enormous amounts of electricity — both to run the hardware and to cool it. A single large hyperscale facility can consume as much power as a small city. In 2022, global data centres consumed an estimated 200–250 TWh of electricity.

The rapid expansion of AI is accelerating this dramatically. Training a single large language model can emit as much CO₂ as five transatlantic flights. Inference (running the model for queries) adds further ongoing energy costs at massive scale.

WASTE

Electronic Waste (E-Waste)

The global production of e-waste reached 53.6 million metric tonnes in 2019 and is projected to double by 2050. Computers, phones, and other electronics contain toxic materials including lead, mercury, and cadmium. When disposed of improperly — as the vast majority are — these leach into soil and groundwater.

A significant proportion of e-waste is exported to lower-income countries where informal recycling workers, often without protection, are exposed to hazardous chemicals. Only about 17% of e-waste is formally recycled globally.

WATER

Water Consumption for Cooling

Many data centres use water-based cooling systems to manage heat. A large facility can consume millions of litres of water per day. In water-stressed regions, this creates direct competition with local communities and agriculture. Microsoft, Google and Meta have all faced scrutiny over their data centre water usage as they expand AI infrastructure.

MINING

Resource Extraction for Hardware

Modern electronics require rare earth elements such as cobalt, lithium, tantalum, and neodymium. Mining these materials causes habitat destruction, soil degradation, and significant water pollution. Lithium mining in the Atacama Desert, for example, depletes underground water reserves in one of the world's driest regions. The push for ever-faster hardware means these pressures intensify with each product cycle.

"The internet is not a cloud. It is a physical infrastructure of cables, servers, and cooling systems that draws more electricity than some entire countries."

■ PART TWO

Towards Greener
Computing

The problems are serious — but so is the ingenuity being directed at solving them. From renewable-powered data centres to circular hardware design, change is underway.

Industry progress on renewable energy

Several major tech companies have committed to running data centres on 100% renewable energy, either by sourcing clean electricity directly or by purchasing Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs). While commitments vary in ambition and accounting method, the overall trend is significant.

What needs to happen

01

Adopt green-by-default software practices

Software engineers increasingly have a role in sustainability. Writing efficient code, avoiding unnecessary computation, choosing cloud regions powered by renewable energy, and designing for longevity rather than obsolescence all reduce environmental impact. Concepts like "green software engineering" are gaining traction as a discipline.

02

Right to repair and extended product lifespans

Policies that require devices to be repairable, that mandate spare parts availability, and that ban planned obsolescence can dramatically reduce e-waste. The EU's Ecodesign Regulation is a step in this direction. Consumers buying second-hand and refurbished electronics also help extend hardware lifetimes.

03

Improve recycling infrastructure and accountability

Formal, certified recycling must become accessible globally — not just in wealthy nations. Producer responsibility legislation, which holds manufacturers accountable for end-of-life product disposal, is proving effective in some regions and should be adopted more widely.

04

Invest in energy-efficient AI hardware

AI chips and architectures are improving in energy efficiency rapidly. Neuromorphic chips, photonic computing, and in-memory computing are research directions that could dramatically reduce the energy cost per computation. Research investment here is critical given the pace of AI deployment.

05

Transparency and carbon accounting

Tech companies should be required to disclose full lifecycle carbon footprints — including scope 3 emissions from hardware manufacturing. Voluntary pledges without accountability mechanisms are insufficient. Mandatory reporting frameworks, already under development in the EU and US, are essential.

"Sustainable computing isn't a constraint on innovation — it is itself one of the most important engineering challenges of our era."
